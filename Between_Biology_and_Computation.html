Between Biology and Computation:

From the perspective of data science few topics offer so much as biology. Today nothing of such complexity is so accessible as life. Our intuitive understanding of tissues and species, coupled with the recent discovery of the details underlying heredity and cellular differentiation, makes a rich stage for data science and "big data".  Now it should be noted that fields like physics, astronomy, and social science all offer great sources of data as well. Yet there seems to be a parallel between knowledge of the biological mechanisms and our mastery of computational techniques over the past several centuries.

Let's start in the 17th century, "Surviving notes from Wilhelm Schickard in 1623 report that he designed and had built the earliest of the modern attempts at mechanizing calculation."(Wikipedia) In the same century Galileo refined the telescope and later in 1667 Robert Hooke published Micrographia, putting microscopy in the lime light. Opening a window into the microscopic world Hooke paved the way for many biologists to follow.

In the 18th century naturalist explorers provided evidence to diversity of life and environments around the world. Although the cash registrar wouldn't be invented until later in the 19th century, 1879 in particular,  the 18th century brought the invention of the stopwatch, and a number of other mechanical revolutions such as steam powered machines (1698).

The 18th and 19th century was the period in human history that most resembles a steam punk universe. The mechanical ingenuity of the 1700's made way for theindustrial revolution which characteristically dominates the later half of the 18th and beginning of the 19th century. In addition to the 19th century bringing machines into the majority of  manufacturing this century also saw the likes of Gregor Mendel (1822-1884) and Charles Darwin (1809-1882).

The First Piece of the Puzzle

Mendel is often regarded as the founder of genetics for his work describing the ratio of traits inherited by progeny of controlled matings of pea plants. Gregor Mendel published his work in 1860. It did not become widely known however until "Independent rediscovery of the laws of inheritance by three botanists--Hugo de Vries (1848-1935), Carl Correns (1864-1935), and Erik Tschermak (1871-1962)"  (ic.galegroup). In the same century while Mendel was working out some of the finer details, Charles Darwin published On the Origin of Species which outlines the idea of evolution by selection upon reproductive fitness. At this point we had developed a firm understanding of the macroscopic patterns presented by procreating life.

Alas there is still so much to be discovered. "The first insight into the relationship between genes and enzymes came in 1909 when it was realized that the inherited human disease phenylketonuria (see Molecular Medicine in Chapter 2) results from a genetic defect in metabolism of the amino acid phenylalanine." (ncbi.nlm.nih.gov) Three years later Alan Turing was born. Turing would go on to work, as did a flock of 20th century mathematicians and scientists, to formally address the capacities of computers and computation. In October of 1948, 2 years after the creation of ENIAC and 3 years before Rosalind Franklin entered Kings College where she would present  findings empowering Watson and Crick to pin down the structure of DNA, Claude Shannonpublished A Mathematical Theory of Communication.  These findings describe theoretical limits to the transmission information. Now entering the age of digital computation we see data science applied in biology. Within the past few years with the advent of high quality microfluidics, digital imaging and computer vision we have achieved technologies that allow us to rapidly digitize the information contained within a cell.

In 1970 Crick published Central Dogma Of Molecular Biology. Here we get our general understanding of the flow of information from DNA to RNA into Protein. An often used paradigm goes like this. DNA is library of plans that builders (ribosomes) can use to make structures (proteins). When the librarian (RNA-Polymerase) checks out records from the library (genes) a blueprint copy (RNA) is made and which can be used by none or more workers (ribosomes) before deteriorating. Each cell has it's own copy of the library (genome) and similarly it's own quantities of specific instructions (RNA) and structures (Proteins). I could continue the analogy calling amino acids building materials and the cell's nucleolus city hall (which contains the library) etc... but I would rather focus on drawing one final connection between biology and computation.

Today transcriptome (an onto mapping of Genes to measured RNA quantity) can be obtained from a single cell and thousands of such samples can be stored in a file smaller than your average music library. (Less than a gigabyte) As a data scientist this is exciting because this data is effectively a spreadsheet, rows of cells and columns of genetic activity. Even without extra columns that indicate extra genetic information (tissue type etc...) this sort of data is incredible. My computer can read a file like this off the disc in about a minute using python, and I'm not computing on anything too special. (I think you could buy my laptop for about $500)  For the computer scientists out there that means that n = a couple thousand and O(n) = n -> about a minute. So you can extrapolate that O(n^2) -> twenty minutes * a constant. Thankfully computers that are much more powerful than mine running languages much faster than python can make light work of this sort of analysis.

To summarize I am excited. We have come so far in so little time, and I can't wait to see what I can do to bring us further along this track in the short time I'm here.
